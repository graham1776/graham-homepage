The First Day: What to Expect

Having your baby unexpectedly admitted to the NICU can be overwhelming. This guide will help you navigate the critical first 24 hours, understand what's happening with your baby's care, and establish your role as an essential member of your baby's care team.

Remember:

  • It's normal to feel shocked, scared, and unprepared
  • The medical team has specific priorities to stabilize your baby
  • You have important priorities too, including self-care
  • This guide will walk you through what to expect, step by step

Immediate Steps When You Arrive

1

Meet Your Baby's Nurse

Ask who is directly caring for your baby. This person will be your main point of contact during their shift and can explain what's happening in real-time.

Ask: "Who is my baby's primary nurse for this shift? When does the shift change?"

2

Get a Status Update

Request a brief explanation of your baby's current condition, what treatments are being given, and immediate concerns.

Ask: "Can you tell me how my baby is doing right now? What are the main concerns at this moment?"

3

Start Documentation

Begin writing down information, including your baby's measurements, diagnoses mentioned, and the names of care providers you meet.

Tip: Use your phone to take notes, or ask if the NICU has parent notebooks available.

4

Learn Visiting Policies

Understand when you can visit, rules about hand washing, and whether other family members can visit.

Ask: "What are the visiting hours? How do we get access to the NICU? What are the rules for siblings or grandparents?"

5

Take a Photo (If Permitted)

Having an image of your baby can help you feel connected when you're not physically present.

Ask: "May I take a photo of my baby? I'd like to have an image to look at when I'm not here."

Understanding Medical Priorities

During the first 24 hours, the medical team has specific priorities focused on stabilizing your baby.

Breathing & Oxygen

The team will ensure your baby is receiving adequate oxygen and support their breathing as needed.

  • Monitoring oxygen saturation levels
  • Providing respiratory support if needed
  • Adjusting oxygen levels based on baby's needs

Temperature Regulation

Maintaining a stable body temperature is crucial, especially for premature babies.

  • Using incubators or radiant warmers
  • Monitoring core temperature regularly
  • Adjusting environmental temperature as needed

Fluids & Nutrition

Ensuring your baby receives adequate hydration and nutrients.

  • IV fluids to maintain hydration
  • Glucose (sugar) monitoring
  • Planning for nutrition (IV nutrition or feeding)

Infection Prevention

Protecting your vulnerable baby from potential infections.

  • Strict hand hygiene protocols
  • Possible preventative antibiotics
  • Sterile procedures for all invasive care

A Nurse's Perspective

"During these first hours, we're focused on stabilizing your baby's vital systems. Each action and assessment gives us important information. What might look like 'doing nothing' is actually careful monitoring and fine-tuning of support based on how your baby responds. We're constantly evaluating, even when we're just standing at the bedside observing."

Common Equipment You'll See

The NICU contains specialized equipment that can look overwhelming at first. Understanding what each device does can help reduce anxiety.

Cardiorespiratory Monitor

Tracks heart rate, breathing rate, and oxygen levels. Connected to your baby via small adhesive pads (leads) on the chest and a small sensor (pulse oximeter) typically on the hand or foot.

Nurse Note: "Alarms sound frequently and don't always mean an emergency. Watch how the nurses respond to understand the urgency. Some alarms might be due to movement or a loose sensor, while others need immediate attention."

Incubator/Isolette

A clear plastic enclosed bed that maintains temperature, humidity, and oxygen levels while allowing observation and access to your baby.

Nurse Note: "You can reach in through the portholes on the sides. Even with all the equipment, touch is important. Ask your nurse about 'hand hugging' or containment holding if your baby is too fragile for being picked up yet."

Respiratory Support

May include oxygen by nasal cannula (small tubes at the nostrils), CPAP (continuous positive airway pressure), or ventilator (breathing machine).

Nurse Note: "Ask which type of support your baby is receiving and why. The goal is always to provide just enough support while encouraging your baby to breathe independently as much as safely possible."

IV Lines

Thin tubes delivering fluids, nutrition, and medications directly into the bloodstream.

Nurse Note: "IVs in babies may be placed in locations that surprise parents: scalp veins, umbilical cord, or tiny hand or foot veins. The location is chosen based on accessibility of veins, not because one spot is better than another."

Feeding Tube

A small flexible tube that goes through the nose or mouth into the stomach to provide nutrition when a baby cannot yet feed by mouth.

Nurse Note: "Even with a feeding tube, oral care with your expressed colostrum or milk can be beneficial. Ask your nurse about this possibility."

Important Questions to Ask in the First 24 Hours

Don't be afraid to ask questions. The medical team expects and welcomes them. Here are key questions to help you understand your baby's condition and care.

About Your Baby's Condition

  • What is my baby's diagnosis or current medical condition?
  • How severe is it, and what is the expected course?
  • What tests have been done and what did they show?
  • What tests will be done in the next 24 hours?

About Your Baby's Care Team

  • Who is my baby's attending physician or neonatologist?
  • Will my baby have primary nurses assigned?
  • When do the care teams round (discuss my baby's case)?
  • Can I be present during rounds?

About Your Involvement

  • How can I be involved in my baby's care today?
  • When can I hold my baby or do skin-to-skin contact?
  • How should I start providing milk if I plan to breastfeed?
  • What can I bring from home for my baby?

About Logistics and Communication

  • What are the visiting policies, including for siblings and grandparents?
  • How do I get updates when I'm not here? Is it okay to call at night?
  • Are there parent accommodations in or near the hospital?
  • What resources are available to help with transportation, meals, etc.?

Tracking Answers

Write down the answers you receive. The first 24 hours involve a lot of information, and it's normal to forget details when under stress. A dedicated notebook or the Notes app on your phone can be invaluable.

First Interactions With Your Baby

Connecting with your baby in the NICU might look different than you imagined, but these early interactions are important for both of you.

Touch

Even the gentlest touch communicates your presence to your baby. Ask your nurse about appropriate ways to touch your baby based on their condition.

Try "hand hugging" by cupping your hands gently around your baby's head and feet, providing containment.

Voice

Your baby knows your voice from hearing it in the womb. Speaking or singing softly can be soothing.

If you're uncomfortable talking in the NICU environment, try reading a simple children's book aloud.

Skin-to-Skin Holding

If your baby is stable enough, skin-to-skin contact (also called kangaroo care) provides tremendous benefits.

Ask when skin-to-skin might be possible, even if it's not immediately. Having something to look forward to can help.

Scent

Your baby recognizes your unique scent, which can be comforting in the unfamiliar NICU environment.

Ask if you can leave a small cloth that you've held against your skin near your baby's head when you're not there.

"I remember a father who was terrified to touch his tiny 26-week preemie. I showed him how to place one hand on her head and one on her feet, providing gentle containment. The baby's vital signs immediately stabilized, and you could see the father's confidence grow as he realized his touch was medicine for his daughter." — Emma, NICU Nurse

Taking Care of Yourself in the First 24 Hours

Your baby needs you to be physically and emotionally well. Self-care isn't selfish—it's necessary.

Rest When Possible

The birth experience plus NICU admission is physically and emotionally exhausting. Your body needs rest to recover.

Even a 20-minute power nap or quiet rest with your eyes closed can help restore your energy.

Stay Hydrated and Nourished

It's easy to forget to eat and drink when worried about your baby, but your physical health is important.

Ask if the NICU has a parent refrigerator where you can store snacks and drinks, or locate the nearest cafeteria.

Process Your Emotions

The range of emotions in the first 24 hours can be overwhelming—fear, grief, guilt, hope, love, and more.

Take a few minutes to write down how you're feeling, or share your feelings with someone you trust.

Connect with Support

You don't have to manage this alone. Reach out to family, friends, or professional support.

Ask if the hospital has a NICU parent support specialist or social worker you can talk to.

"I didn't sleep the first night my twins were in the NICU. By the next day, I was so exhausted I couldn't focus on what the doctors were saying. A nurse gently told me, 'The best thing you can do for your babies right now is to get 4 hours of uninterrupted sleep.' She was right. After sleeping, I could think clearly and be more present for my babies." — Jen, mother of 29-week twins
